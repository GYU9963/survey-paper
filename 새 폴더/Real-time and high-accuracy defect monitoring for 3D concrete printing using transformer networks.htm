Abstract

Defects and anomalies during the 3D concrete printing (3DCP) process significantly affect final construction quality. This paper proposes a real-time, high-accuracy method for monitoring defects in the printing process using a transformer-based detector. Despite limited data availability, deep learning-based data augmentation and image processing techniques were employed to enable effective training of this complex transformer model. A range of enhancement strategies was applied to the RT-DETR, resulting in remarkable improvements, including a mAP50 of 98.1Â %, mAP50â€“95 of 68.0Â %, and a computation speed of 72 FPS. The enhanced RT-DETR outperformed state-of-the-art detectors such as YOLOv8 and YOLOv7 in detecting defects in 3DCP. Furthermore, the improved RT-DETR was used to analyze the relationships between defect count, size, and printer parameters, providing guidance for operators to fine-tune printer settings and promptly address defects. This monitoring method reduces material waste and minimizes the risk of structural collapse during the printing process.

Introduction

Traditional construction methodologies face challenges regarding high costs and human dependency, low manufacturing accuracy, and design diversity [1,2]. As a result, additive manufacturing technology is proposed to overcome current industrial issues, bringing along modified construction automation and design flexibility [3,4]. Waste production, construction time, and labor costs are respectively reduced by 60Â %, 50Â %â€“70Â %, and 50Â %â€“80Â % when additive manufacturing replaces traditional construction [[5], [6], [7]]. Therefore, additive manufacturing in construction would also significantly help humans build residential structures on the moon [3,8].
3D concrete printing (3DCP) is an automated construction method that utilizes layer-by-layer deposition based on computer-aided design (CAD) models to create intricate 3D structures [9,10]. However, achieving high-quality printing demands a stable printing process that harmonizes multiple critical factors, including material, equipment, and environment [2,11]. 3D printed concrete is prone to certain defects such as breakpoints, voids, and pits primarily due to its non-framework craft, rapid drying, fast-hydration characteristics, and the printing process with deposition and extrusion [[12], [13], [14]]. These inherent limitations can significantly impact the mechanical properties of the printed components [11,15]. Particularly, breakpoint defects, the completely disconnecting cross-section of extruded filaments during the printing process, lead to an unsuccessful printing of a structure, material waste, and even safety risks. The occurrence of defects is influenced by various factors, such as the mechanical and rheological properties of the material fresh state, the geometric dimensions of the printer nozzle, printer parameters, and material mix design [16]. Among the various factors, printer parameters, mainly including the extrusion speed of material and movement speed of the printer nozzle, are the only ones that allow for flexible adjustments during the printing process without prolonged interruption [17]. Therefore, continuous monitoring of anomalies and real-time adjustments for printer parameters are vital, significantly enhancing the likelihood of flawless and structurally sound components.
In the past, 3DCP quality monitoring and controlling heavily relied on artificial visual anomaly detection and diagnosis synchronization between the speed of extruding materials and moving velocity of the printer nozzle [5,18,19]. Furthermore, the non-real-time measurements of rheological properties, air content, and temperature serve as supplementary methods [6,20,21]. In addition, monitoring and evaluating the cause of the anomalies demand an experienced human operator to manually readjust suitable parameters and resume printing [22]. Nevertheless, this traditional approach suffers from several limitations, including low efficiency, over-reliance on individual experience, subjective impact, and the inability to monitor areas beyond the view field of operator [[22], [23], [24]]. Moreover, it has proven to be high-cost, inaccurate, off-line, time-consuming, and insufficient for large-scale and building-level 3DCP [22,25]. Therefore, there is a growing need for real-time and automated monitoring and control methods to overcome these challenges.

    • Co-driven physics and machine learning for intelligent control in high-precision 3D concrete printing

      2025, Automation in Construction
      With the increasing demand for precise control in 3D concrete printing, coordinating material rheological properties and printing parameters has become a critical challenge. This paper addresses how to intelligently optimize printing parameters to adapt to varying concrete material attributes and improve printing quality. A dual-path framework co-driven by physical information equations (PIE) and machine learning (ML) is proposed. PIE is embedded into convolutional neural networks (CNN) to enhance rheological properties prediction, while also coupled with the random forest (RF) model to predict printing parameters. Results show this approach efficiently matches yield stress (YS), plastic viscosity (PV), extrusion speed (ES), and printing speed (PS), significantly enhancing printing performance. This research provides construction engineers and 3D printing operators with a physics-guided, interpretable intelligent tool that reduces trial-and-error and improves construction reliability. The integration strategy also opens promising directions for future studies on large-scale printing involving multi-scale material-process-structure optimization and time-dependent rheological modeling.

    • Automated analysis system for micro-defects in 3D printed concrete

      2025, Automation in Construction
      The internal micro-defects of 3D printed concrete (3DPC) play a pivotal role in influencing its mechanical properties. Nonetheless, the acquisition of representative internal micro-defect information is hindered by computational inefficiencies and quantification limitations of the current equipment system. This paper proposes a deep learning based system to assist SEM equipment in automatically quantifying micro-defects of 3DPC for in-depth microstructural analysis that surpasses traditional SEM methods. Through optimal resizing approach and model enhancement tactics, the proposed micro-defect segmentation model leverages advantages of both convolutional neural networks and transformer. This improvement segmentation capability achieves higher accuracy and faster speed than current algorithms, enabling system to achieve accurate quantitative analyses of micro-defects. Using this automated analysis system, the relationship among micro-defect areas in 3DPC, mechanical properties, and printer parameters is investigated. Therefore, the proposed system reduces labour and computational time, demonstrating significant potential for applications in analyzing concrete microstructure.

    • Microcrack investigations of 3D printing concrete using multiple transformer networks

      2025, Automation in Construction
      Extrusion-filament and no-framework craft significantly influence microcracks in 3D printing concrete (3DPC). A detailed analysis of these microcracks is essential to improve overall performance of material. However, fast and automated methods for capturing and measuring representative microcrack information in 3DPC are currently lacking. This paper presents a transformer based method for automatic quantization of microcosmic information in 3DPC, enabling a comprehensive analysis of microcracks. Additionally, a transformer network to rapidly and cost-effectively obtain high-quality microscopic images is introduced. The proposed quantization method involves a range of enhancement tactics over an existing baseline model, demonstrating higher accuracy in detecting inner microcracks of 3DPC compared to current advanced algorithms. This method surpasses existing microscopic imaging technologies in terms of information content, computational speed, and cost-efficiency. Therefore, this method will have promising applications for analyzing other micro-details in concrete when it is supplemented with a diverse and extensive training dataset.
